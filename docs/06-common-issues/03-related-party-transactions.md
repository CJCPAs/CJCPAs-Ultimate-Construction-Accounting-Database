# Related Party Transactions in Construction

## Overview

Related party transactions are prevalent in construction due to common ownership structures, family businesses, and the use of multiple entities for different functions.

## Common Related Party Structures

### Typical Construction Related Parties

```
RELATED PARTY IDENTIFICATION

Ownership-Related:
- Principal shareholders/members
- Family members of owners
- Entities owned by shareholders
- Trusts benefiting owners

Management-Related:
- Officers and directors
- Key employees
- Family members of management

Affiliated Entities:
- Equipment holding companies
- Real estate entities
- Supply companies
- Subcontracting entities
- Joint ventures

Other:
- Pension plans
- Employee benefit trusts
- Bonding company principals
```

## Common Related Party Transactions

### Equipment Leasing

```
RELATED PARTY EQUIPMENT LEASE

Structure:
- Equipment LLC owns heavy equipment
- Equipment LLC owned by same persons as Construction Co.
- Construction Co. leases equipment from Equipment LLC

Audit/Accounting Issues:
□ Are lease rates at arm's length?
□ Is there proper lease documentation?
□ Should transactions be eliminated?
□ Are there tax implications?

Testing Procedures:
1. Compare rates to market (dealer quotes, rental companies)
2. Review lease agreements
3. Evaluate substance of transactions
4. Consider consolidation requirements
```

### Real Estate Arrangements

```
RELATED PARTY REAL ESTATE

Common Scenarios:
- Office/yard leased from owner's entity
- Construction on owner's property
- Equipment storage facilities

Testing Procedures:
1. Obtain lease agreements
2. Compare to market rates (appraisal, comps)
3. Review payment terms
4. Evaluate for proper disclosure
```

### Intercompany Services

| Service Type | Audit Consideration |
|-------------|---------------------|

### Loans and Advances

```
RELATED PARTY LOANS

From Company to Related Party:
- Loans to shareholders
- Advances to affiliates
- Notes receivable from officers

Audit Considerations:
□ Is there proper documentation?
□ Are terms at market?
□ Is collectibility assured?
□ Is there board approval?
□ Proper balance sheet presentation?

From Related Party to Company:
- Shareholder loans
- Advances from affiliates
- Capital contributions disguised as loans

Audit Considerations:
□ Is documentation adequate?
□ Debt vs. equity classification
□ Interest rate appropriate?
□ Subordination agreements?
```

## Identification Procedures

### Inquiry Process

```
RELATED PARTY IDENTIFICATION INQUIRIES

To Management:
□ Names of all owners, officers, directors
□ Family relationships among management
□ Other entities owned by principals
□ Transactions with any affiliated entities
□ Loans to/from owners or affiliates
□ Guarantees involving related parties
□ Any side agreements with related parties

To Accounting Staff:
□ Unusual vendor or customer relationships
□ Transactions without normal documentation
□ Payments to individuals (not companies)
□ Recurring transactions with same parties
```

### Documentation Review

```
RELATED PARTY DOCUMENTATION REVIEW

Sources to Review:
□ Corporate minutes
□ Entity organizational documents
□ Shareholder/operating agreements
□ Tax returns (Schedules K-1, related party)
□ Insurance policies (named insureds)
□ Loan agreements (guarantors)
□ Bonding applications
□ Lease agreements
□ Management agreements
```

## Audit Procedures

### Testing Related Party Transactions

```
RELATED PARTY AUDIT PROCEDURES

1. Identification
   □ Inquire of management
   □ Review corporate records
   □ Search for common addresses
   □ Review vendor/customer master files

2. Understanding
   □ Document business purpose
   □ Understand nature of relationship
   □ Assess risks of transactions

3. Testing
   □ Verify pricing is at arm's length
   □ Review supporting documentation
   □ Confirm significant transactions
   □ Evaluate authorization

4. Evaluation
   □ Assess appropriateness of accounting
   □ Evaluate disclosure adequacy
   □ Consider fraud implications
```

### Arm's Length Evaluation

| Transaction | Comparison Basis |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Equipment lease | Third-party rental rates |
| Real estate lease | Market rent appraisal |
| Services | Market rate for services |
| Loans | Prevailing interest rates |
| Sales | Market prices |

## Disclosure Requirements

### Required Disclosures

Under GAAP, disclose:
- Nature of relationship
- Description of transactions
- Dollar amounts
- Amounts due to/from
- Terms and manner of settlement

### Sample Disclosure

```
NOTE X - RELATED PARTY TRANSACTIONS

The Company leases certain equipment from XYZ Equipment LLC, an 
entity owned by the shareholders of the Company. Lease expense 
under this arrangement was $450,000 for the year ended 
December 31, 2024. Management believes the lease rates are 
comparable to rates charged by third-party equipment rental 
companies.

The Company leases its office and yard facilities from XYZ 
Properties LLC, an entity owned by the shareholders. Annual 
rent is $180,000. Management believes this rate is comparable 
to market rates for similar properties.

The Company owed XYZ Equipment LLC $125,000 and XYZ Properties 
LLC $15,000 at December 31, 2024, for equipment and facility 
rent, respectively.

The Company has a note receivable of $200,000 from an officer 
of the Company. The note bears interest at 5% and is due 
December 31, 2026.
```

## Red Flags

### Warning Signs

```
RELATED PARTY RED FLAGS

Structural Red Flags:
□ Complex ownership structures
□ Frequent entity formations
□ Entities with no apparent business purpose
□ Shell companies in ownership chain

Transaction Red Flags:
□ Transactions without business substance
□ Prices significantly above/below market
□ Unusual or complex terms
□ Transactions near period end
□ Round dollar amounts
□ Missing documentation

Behavioral Red Flags:
□ Reluctance to disclose relationships
□ Inconsistent information provided
□ Changes in story when questioned
□ Missing or altered documents
```

## Special Considerations

### Joint Ventures

- Often involve related parties
- May have complex allocation arrangements
- Consider consolidation requirements
- Evaluate management fee arrangements

### Affiliated Subcontractors

- Common to subcontract to related entities
- May affect bonding capacity
- Evaluate pricing reasonableness
- Consider disclosure implications

### Personal Guarantees

- Shareholders often guarantee company obligations
- May indicate financial strength or weakness
- Disclose significant guarantees
- Consider going concern implications
